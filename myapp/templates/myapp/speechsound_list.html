What is Phonological Awareness?

Phonological awareness is an understanding that words are composed of sound units, including syllables, onsets, and rimes. This foundational skill is essential for reading and literacy as it helps children recognize the structure and patterns of spoken language.

Skills associated with phonological awareness include recognizing rhyming words, clapping out syllables, and identifying the initial or final sounds in words. Building phonological awareness often involves games, songs, and activities that encourage children to listen to and play with the sounds in words.

What is Phonemic Awareness?

Phonemic awareness is the specific ability to focus on and manipulate individual phonemes, or sounds, in spoken words. This skill allows children to recognize that the word "cat" is composed of three distinct sounds: /k/, /a/, and /t/.

Developing phonemic awareness helps children with both decoding (sounding out words) and spelling as they learn the correspondence between sounds and letters. Activities such as blending, segmenting, and manipulating phonemes are commonly used to foster phonemic awareness in early readers.

How to Teach Phonemic Awareness

  • Sound Matching: Engage children by asking them to identify words with the same initial sound, such as "bat" and "ball," to build sound recognition.
  • Sound Isolation: Help children isolate the first, middle, or last sounds in simple words, for example, "What is the first sound in 'dog'?"
  • Sound Blending: Practice blending sounds to form words, such as saying "c-a-t" and asking the child to blend them to form "cat".
  • Sound Segmentation: Encourage children to break down words into their individual sounds, for example, "What sounds do you hear in 'sit'?" (s-i-t).
